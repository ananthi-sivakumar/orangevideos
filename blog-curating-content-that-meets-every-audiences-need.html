What This Means for Your Brand

Every video production decision carries strategic weight. The format you choose, the platform you optimise for, the story you tell — each of these decisions either amplifies or diminishes your brand's ability to connect with the right audience at the right moment.

The brands that win with video aren't necessarily the ones with the biggest budgets. They're the ones with the clearest thinking about what they want their audience to feel, understand, or do after watching. That clarity — more than production value — is what separates the videos that drive results from the ones that disappear into the scroll.

The Orange Videos Perspective

In our experience working with brands across FMCG, BFSI, manufacturing, healthcare, technology, and education sectors, the most effective videos share three characteristics: they have a clear, singular objective; they are built for the specific platform and audience they're targeting; and they are produced with enough creative ambition to stand out in an environment saturated with content.

This is harder to achieve than it sounds. The default for most briefs is to try to do too much — communicate too many messages, reach too many audiences, serve too many stakeholders. The discipline of simplicity is one of the hardest things we help our clients develop.

How to Apply This to Your Next Video Project

Start with the Audience, Not the Product

The most common mistake we see in video briefs is leading with what the brand wants to say rather than what the audience needs to hear. These are rarely the same thing. The best creative work begins with a genuine, specific insight about the audience — what they believe, what they fear, what they aspire to — and builds outward from there.

Choose One Platform and Own It

Multi-platform distribution is a goal for optimisation, not production. When you're making creative decisions, anchor to one primary platform and one primary moment of consumption. A video designed for lean-back YouTube viewing will feel wrong on Instagram. A vertical Reel repurposed for a boardroom presentation will fall flat. Make it right for one context first, then adapt intelligently.

Measure What Matters

Video metrics are easy to collect and easy to misread. Views and impressions are vanity metrics unless tied to a defined objective. If your goal is brand awareness, measure aided and unaided recall. If it's consideration, measure intent lift. If it's conversion, measure downstream sales attribution. Match the metric to the objective, not the other way around.

Conclusion

The gap between brands that use video effectively and those that don't is widening. The tools are more accessible than ever, the distribution channels are more powerful, and the audience appetite for compelling visual content shows no sign of slowing. The question is whether your brand is building the systems, the relationships, and the creative culture to take full advantage.
